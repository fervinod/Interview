Tell me about a time when you advocated for and pushed your own ideas forward despite opposition.

Virtual Machine Power User role, which allowed us to manage VM power states, create snapshots, and modify virtual hardware
Situation:
Our team relied heavily on a VMware ESXi environment that hosted a significant portion of our core infrastructure. The environment was managed entirely by the Windows team, who were responsible for deploying and maintaining virtual machines. Whenever we needed a new VM or had to investigate an issue, we had to raise a request and wait for the Windows team to perform the necessary actions. Our team only had read-only access, which limited our ability to troubleshoot incidents efficiently.
Task:
I identified an opportunity to improve our operational efficiency by obtaining a limited level of access to the VMware environment. However, there was resistance from both the Windows team and within my own team. The Windows team was concerned that granting access could lead to unauthorized changes and potential outages. Internally, there were concerns that gaining access might result in additional infrastructure responsibilities being transferred to our team in the future.
Action:
I advocated for a balanced solution that addressed both concerns. I presented the operational benefits of having limited VMware access, highlighting how it would significantly reduce troubleshooting time, improve incident response, and decrease dependency on another team for routine tasks. To mitigate risk, I proposed that our team be granted only the Virtual Machine Power User role, which allowed us to manage VM power states, create snapshots, and modify virtual hardware when required, while still preventing access to critical infrastructure configuration. I also worked with stakeholders to establish clear boundaries and expectations regarding responsibilities, ensuring that access would not automatically result in ownership of the VMware platform.
Result:
As a result of these discussions, we gained the appropriate level of access needed to support our VM’s more effectively. This reduced delays during troubleshooting, improved collaboration between teams, and enabled faster resolution of incidents affecting our services. By proposing a controlled and low-risk approach, I was able to gain stakeholder support despite the initial opposition and drive a change that improved operational efficiency without compromising governance or security.

Handover issue

Situation:
In a previous role, our shifts concluded with a handover call attended by team members, team leads, and occasionally managers. During my first year with the organization, I noticed that a senior colleague would frequently interrupt me during these calls and take over the discussion, particularly when I was presenting updates on critical incidents that had received management attention. As a result, I felt that my contributions were not being fully recognized.
Task:
I wanted to ensure that I could effectively communicate the work I had completed and demonstrate my capabilities to leadership, while also maintaining a positive working relationship with my colleague, who had been supportive during my onboarding and transition into the team.
Action:
Rather than escalating the issue or confronting him publicly, I decided to address it directly and respectfully. I invited him for a coffee break and shared my concerns privately. I first thanked him for the support he had provided since I joined the team and then explained that, as a newer team member, it was important for me to have opportunities to present the incidents I had worked on. I asked whether he could allow me to complete my updates during handover calls and then add any additional context or corrections afterward if needed. Initially, he was somewhat defensive, but I remained calm and focused on explaining how this would help me demonstrate my growth and ability to handle issues independently.
Result:
After our discussion, he became much more mindful during handover calls and gave me the space to present my work. This improved my visibility with team leads and managers and helped me build confidence in communicating technical updates. Most importantly, we maintained a strong professional relationship and continued to work well together.
What I Learned:
This experience taught me that workplace conflicts are often best resolved through direct, respectful communication rather than assumptions or escalation. I learned that success is not only about delivering results but also about building trust and strong relationships. By approaching the situation with empathy and professionalism, I was able to resolve the issue while strengthening the working relationship.

Q: Tell me about a time you handled a critical production issue in a data center.
PDU failed it a partial outage frame the root cause and impact
Situation:
We experienced a partial data center outage when a critical PDU (Power Distribution Unit) failed. The PDU was responsible for distributing power to a specific set of racks, so the failure only affected equipment connected to that power path rather than the entire facility.
Root Cause:
The root cause was a hardware failure within the PDU, which interrupted power delivery to the affected racks. Although the data center had redundancy, some equipment was either connected to the impacted power path or did not fail over as expected, resulting in service disruption for a subset of systems.
Impact:
The outage affected several servers and services hosted in the impacted racks, leading to temporary service degradation and increased operational risk. Internal teams had to respond quickly to restore power and minimize customer impact.
Actions Taken:
I coordinated with DC team, operations, and  vendor teams to identify the failed PDU, isolate the issue, and restore services. We conducted a root cause analysis, validated failover procedures, reviewed power-path dependencies, and implemented additional monitoring and maintenance checks to reduce the likelihood of a similar incident.
I engaged our incident management team to engage all the affected stakeholds, which is the app teams and started the recovery procedure.

Result:
Power was restored within the recovery target, affected services were brought back online, and we identified improvements to our redundancy validation process. The incident reinforced the importance of regularly testing failover mechanisms and understanding power dependencies across critical infrastructure.
